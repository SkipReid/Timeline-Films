Victorian Villains

Victorian Villains reveals for the first time, the fascinating photographs and compelling stories of the young Scots sent to prison in the 19th century.

About the show

Until the mid 1800’s, the only records of young offenders were written accounts of their trials, but then a revolutionary new invention changed everything; photography. By the 1870’s every criminal – young and old, had their picture taken for police records, and many of these photographs still exist, giving us a remarkable insight into the harsh reality of being a young Victorian in Scotland.

Using trial testimony, census records, and dramatic reconstruction to recreate the moment when these children’s lives were changed forever, we tell the stories of the "young Victorian villains" and the strict justice system that decided their fate. At the heart of the programme are the powerful photographic images of these youngsters, which give a poignancy and authenticity to their stories, and bring their tales, quite literally, to life.

  • James Dunn Barr 1889

    The true story of 14 year old James Dunn Barr who was was sentenced to 9 months in prison for housebreaking.

    James, from Glasgow, reveals to us that he has a number of previous convictions for theft which is why he has been given such a harsh sentence. He also tells us about the experience of getting his photograph taken for police records and explains why Victorian criminals were required to show their hands to the camera.

  • James Fleming 1877

    The true story of 13 year old James Fleming who tried to burn down the Industrial School he had been sent to.

    In Victorian times, young orphans, beggars and vagrants would be sent to Industrial Schools to learn a trade, to try and stop poor children falling into a life of crime. James did not however enjoy life in the Industrial School he was sent to and admits that he and a friend tried to burn it down. James tells us how he and his accomplice George Fleming were caught and convicted of "reckless fire raising" and as a result are to be sent to an even stricter institution – a reformatory school – for three years.

    • Watch on the BBC2 website

  • Jane Angus 1873

    The true story of 12 year old Jane Angus who was sent to prison and then Reformatory School after being caught stealing.

    These strict residential schools were meant to teach young criminals a trade and stop them re-offending, and Jane gives us an insight into what her daily routine at Reformatory is like.

    After spending 5 years at Reformatory School, Jane tells us how she was released, but then contracted Tuberculosis, known in Victorian times as "Consumption" and a major killer at that time. Jane died in 1879 at the age of just 17.

    • Watch on the BBC2 website

  • John Smith 1876

    The true story of 14 year old farmer worker John Smith who found himself sent to prison for 6 months for forging a letter from his employer.

    John gives us an insight into the kind of punishments which were frequently used in Victorian times. Picking oakham, being put to "the crank," being whipped and even being transported to Australia were all punishments that young criminals would have been subjected to in the 1800’s.
